Victimization Retreat

Charles Alexander Asbury's wife prefers to call it a spa, but the brochure was clear: The Victimization Retreat for Chronically Privileged Males. It is a three-story brick Georgian housed in a pine forest at the end of a peninsula, two miles from the nearest paved road.

"You'll be fabulous," she tells him. "Until then, fake it." Anita’s teeth are perfectly white. Her dress is Saint Laurent.

She leaves him at the gate, her SUV kicking up a cloud of red dust that smells of pine and iron. Charles stands with his leather suitcase, his linen trousers already wrinkled from the drive, his silk shirt sticking to his ribs. He is fifty-five, a retired partner in a firm that deals in mergers and acquisitions, and he has never been anywhere without a reservation.

The director, a woman named Carrie who wears a gray linen dress and has a face like a dry pear, welcomes him.

"We have thirty-four guests this month, Charles," she says, leading him up the steps. The house smells of lavender and floor wax. "All of them are leaders in their fields: finance, law, medicine, politics. All of them are experiencing a sense of... displacement."

"My wife says I have an attitude problem," Charles says.

"A common diagnosis," Carrie says. She shows him his room: a green iron bed, a wooden cross on the yellow wall, bars on the window. "Your agenda is on the table."

The retreat is run on a strict schedule.

Six-thirty, coffee. Seven to eight-thirty, yardwork. Nine to ten-thirty, cleaning the bathrooms. Eleven to twelve-thirty, group therapy, which Carrie calls the Litany of Grievances.

The guests sit in a circle on green leather chairs, under a spotlight that makes Charles' head ache. Opposite him is a man named Miller, who used to be a judge in Chicago until he was caught taking bribes from a developer.

"My investments are gone," Miller says. He has a red face and hands that shake. "My wife took the house in Lake Forest. I'm living in a condo by the airport. It's... humiliating."

"The humiliation is the treatment," Carrie says from her corner. "You must embrace the wretchedness, Miller. It is your natural state."

Charles doesn't want to embrace anything. He spends his afternoons cleaning the gutters, standing on a thirty-foot ladder while the wind from the canyon rattles the shingles. The wood is dry and crumbles under his fingers, and his hands are blistered and raw.

"You're not using enough pressure," says a voice from below.

It is the caretaker, a young black man named Dennis who wears handyman clothes and has a face like a quiet map of the south. He stands with his hands in his pockets, watching Charles struggle with the trowel.

"The trowel's dull," Charles says.

"The trowel's fine," Dennis says. He reaches into his pocket and pulls out a tin of chaw, offers it to Charles. "You want some?"

"No," Charles says.

"Suit yourself," Dennis says. He spits in the sumac. "Your father's well was dry, wasn't it?"

"My father was a banker," Charles says. "He didn't have a well."

"Everybody's father had a well," Dennis says, and walks off.

~

The fire started on Tuesday, in the forest behind the garage.

A dry storm had passed through, the lightning striking a dead pine, and within an hour, the air was full of smoke and the smell of burning pitch. The guests gathered on the lawn, their linen shirts gray with soot, their faces white in the red glow of the forest.

"The firebreak is twenty feet wide," Carrie said. She stood on the porch, her binder open. "The insurance is paid. We should prepare to evacuate."

"We can't evacuate," Miller said. "The road's blocked. A tree fell across the gate."

"Then we dig," Charles said.

The guests looked at him. They were sixty, fifty-five, forty-five; their hands were soft, their boots new, their spines stiff from years in leather chairs. But the smoke was in their lungs, and the heat was rising, a wall of orange that turned the pines to giant torches.

Charles took the spade from Dennis' cart. "Get the shovels," he said.

They spent the night in the ditch, thirty-four rich men plowing a fresh break through the sumac, their back muscles screaming, their hands bleeding through their silk gloves. Miller worked beside Charles, his face purple, his breath a wet rattle, but he didn't stop.

By dawn, the fire had reached the break. It flared, a wall of heat that singed Charles' eyebrows, then died in the dirt, the black ash smoldering on the red clay.

Dennis stood at the edge of the line, his shovel on his shoulder. He looked at the black gap, then at the guests, sitting on the lawn in their ruined Saint Laurent.

"You boys done alright," Dennis said. "Reckon you can go home now."

Anita came for Charles on Friday. The SUV was clean, her teeth white, her dress perfect. She looked at his blistered hands, his singed eyebrows, his gray linen trousers.

"My God, Charles," she said. "You look fabulous."

"I am fabulous," Charles said. He got into the passenger seat, his boots in the clean carpet. "But the flashing's compromised. We need to buy some zinc."

Anita started the engine, her rings glinting in the sun, and as the SUV rolled through the gate, Charles looked back at the house. Dennis was on the roof, his form shiny against the sky, his hand cupped around the neck of his well pump.

"You'll be fabulous," she tells him. "Until then, fake it." Anita’s teeth are perfectly white. Her dress is Saint Laurent.
