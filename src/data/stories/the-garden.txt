The Garden

From this distance she looks like a child, Sadie in her yellow raincoat, her boots too big for her feet, dragging a rusted red wagon through the weeds. She's eighty-two, with a spine like a dry pine branch and hands that can't hold a cup without rattling the saucer, but she won't stay inside.

"The soil's cold," I tell her from the porch.

"The soil's fine," she says. Her voice is like gravel being turned in a shovel. "The carrots need to go in before the frost. If you're not going to help, go make some tea."

I go inside. The house is old, built by her grandfather in the days when the valley was full of orchards instead of warehouses. It smells of dried lavender and damp cellar, a scent I've associated with her since I was ten, when my mother left me here for a summer and forgot to come back.

Through the kitchen window, I watch her spade the earth. She moves with a slow, heavy rhythm, her boots sinking into the black mud, her hands steady when they're working. She's planted forty-two species of rose in this yard, all of them dead now, killed by the salt from the highway run-off. Only the sumac survives, a line of red-eyed shrubs that fences out the interstate.

~

We had a visitor in the afternoon.

A young man in a gray suit, his shoes shiny as coal, carrying a leather binder. He stood at the edge of the garden, his trousers tucked into his socks to keep off the mud.

"Mrs. Alexander?"
"Sadie," she said. She didn't stop digging. "If you're selling Bibles, we got three. If you're from the state, the well's clean."

"I'm from the development corporation," he said. He had a soft, clean voice that suggested he'd never had dirt under his fingernails. "We're representing the logistics park. We're prepared to offer you three hundred thousand for the acre."

Sadie set her spade in the dirt and leaned on the handle. "Three hundred thousand?"

"Yes, ma'am. Cash. You could buy a nice condo in the city. With an elevator. No more gutters to clean."

"I like my gutters," Sadie said. "And I don't like elevators. They smell of other people's hairspray."

"The offer's only good through Friday," the man said. He looked at his watch, a gold circle that winked in the gray light. "The highway's expanding, Mrs. Alexander. The noise will only get worse."

"The noise is fine," she said. "It keeps the crows off the tomatoes. Now go away. You're standing on my parsnips."

~

The frost came on Thursday, a white sheet that covered the valley, turning the sumac to glass. The warehouses across the road were silent, their loading docks white with ice, their big trucks idling in the yard like sleeping bulls.

Sadie didn't get up at six. I went into her room at eight. The room was cold, the heater having failed sometime in the night, and she was small under the quilts, her eyes open, staring at the ceiling.

"Sadie?"

"My legs are gone," she said. Her voice was small, a dry whisper that seemed to come from the closet. "They're just wood. Bring me some tea, Arthur."

I called the doctor, but the road was iced over and he said it would be noon before the sand trucks got out. I built a fire in the parlor, using the old rosewood chairs she'd inherited from her mother, the dry wood catching with a scent of lavender and varnish.

I carried her to the sofa. She was light, her bones like bird bones, her skin translucent in the firelight. We sat together, watching the sumac turn red as the sun came up.

"He was a nice boy," she said.

"Who?"

"The boy in the suit. He had clean ears."

"He wanted your garden, Sadie."

"Everything wants the garden," she said. "The state, the bank, the frost. They all want to dig it up."

She died before the sand trucks arrived, her hand in mine, her fingers tightening once as a truck roared on the highway, its air brakes screaming in the canyon.

~

The man from the development corporation came back on Monday. He stood in the parlor, his shoes clean, his binder open.

"I'm sorry for your loss," he said.

"Thank you."

"The offer still stands," he said. "To you, as the heir. Three hundred thousand."

I looked out the window. The garden was gray and dead, the wagon frozen in the mud, its red paint peeling like scabbed skin. The sumac along the fence was red, their fuzzy berries covered in ice like sugared fruit.

"No," I said.

"It's a good price, Mr. Alexander. The house is worthless. It has radon in the cellar."

"I like the radon," I said.

I spent the afternoon digging. The earth was hard, a crust of ice six inches deep that I had to break with the axe. Below that, the soil was black and wet, smelling of old leaves and rot. I dug a hole six feet deep, behind the sumac where the highway noise is loudest.

I didn't buy a coffin. I wrapped her in her yellow raincoat, her boots still on her feet, and laid her in the red wagon. I wheeled her out to the hole and slid her in, the yellow plastic crinkling in the cold air.

I filled the hole with the black earth, then covered it with the frozen turf. I stood there until the sun went down, a red coal that sank behind the warehouses, and though I knew it was too hot for snow, I ran in the yard, mouth agape, straining to catch the ashes on his tongue. I'll plant Sadie in her garden. Now we close our eyes and turn wet faces to a weak sun.
