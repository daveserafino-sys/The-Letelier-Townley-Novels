Migration Season

Ma's been burning incense and chanting since five in the morning. The smell of sandalwood is thick as wool in the hall, and it makes my nose itch, but I don't say nothing. Today's the day the cranes leave.

We live in a flat over the dry cleaners, three rooms with linoleum floors and windows that overlook the elevated train. Every twenty minutes the house shakes, the teacups rattling in their saucers, the light bulbs swinging on their cords like hanging men. You get used to it. I can tell the time by the vibration: the six-ten is a low rumble, the six-thirty a sharp clatter, the seven-o'clock express a roar that drowns out Ma's chanting.

"Ninh," Ma says. She's sitting on her mat, her hands folded in her lap. Her hair is white and thin, her face dry as paper. She came here thirty years ago on a boat, her family lost in the delta, and she's never quite believed she's here. "The birds are in the north. They smell the cold."

"They're just birds, Ma," I say. I'm putting on my boots. I work at the docks, unloading containers of rice and tea. It's eighty bucks a day, cash, which is enough for rent and Ma's incense.

"They're not just birds," she says. "They are the souls of the dead. They go back to the water."

~

At the docks, the air is cold and smells of diesel and rotting fish. The container ships sit high in the water, their cranes reaching into the gray sky like skeleton fingers. I work with a man named Tran, who has a water buffalo tattooed on his forearm. He's sixty, with teeth like old piano keys and a laugh that sounds like a dry engine.

"Ma's chanting again," I tell him during our break. We're sitting on a stack of pallets, drinking sweet coffee from paper cups.

"Good," Tran says. He spits into the harbor, where a slick of green oil is coating the rocks. "Somebody should pray. This city's going to the dogs."

"You think the cranes are coming?"

"They always come," he says. "Even when the river was full of bodies, they came. They don't care about our wars."

He's right. Every November, the white cranes descend on the marshes behind the industrial park, thousands of them, a white blanket that covers the mud. They stay for a week, feeding on the small crabs and frogs, and then they leave for the south. The city people come in their cars to watch, holding their phones out, trying to catch the white shapes against the gray sky.

~

The fire started in the industrial park on Wednesday. A chemical tank exploded, and by evening, the sky was a hellish yellow, the smoke rising in a column that could be seen from fifty miles away. The air smelled of sulfur and burning plastic, and the dry cleaners downstairs closed their doors to keep the soot from the clothes.

Ma stayed on her mat, her chanting rising over the sound of the sirens. "The water is burning," she said.

"It's just the factory, Ma," I said, but I was scared. The smoke was coming our way, a black cloud that tasted of metal and vinegar.

By Friday, the marshes were on fire. The dry grass burned like paper, and the soot fell on our windows like gray snow. The cranes were still there, trapped in the smoke, their white wings gray with ash. We could hear them from the flat, a wild, metallic trumpeting that rose over the sound of the trains.

"We have to help them," Ma said. She stood up, her legs shaking.

"We can't do nothing, Ma," I said. "The police closed the road."

But she wouldn't listen. She took her basket and went down the stairs, her slippers clacking on the wood. I followed her, my coat over my shoulder, my boots heavy in the soot.

The road to the marshes was blocked by a patrol car, its blue lights winking in the smoke. The cop was a young white guy with a face like a lard bucket. He told us to go back.

"The birds," Ma said. She pointed into the smoke.

"The birds are cooked, lady," the cop said. "Go home."

Ma didn't move. She stood by the barricade, her hands folded, her face white in the blue light. She began to chant, her voice small and sharp against the roar of the fire.

And then they came.

Not from the south, but from the smoke, a white wave that rose from the black grass. They flew low, their wings beating the air with a sound like wet sheets on a line. They passed over us, fifty feet up, their long necks stretched out, their legs trailing behind them like reeds. The soot fell from their feathers in a shower of black sparks, and as they passed, the air smelled of ozone and wet feathers.

Ma held out her hands, her face upturned, her eyes wide. "They're going," she said.

"Yeah," I said.

We watched them until they were gone, a gray line that melted into the gray sky.

~

Ma died that winter. She went to sleep on her mat and didn't wake up. I buried her in the cemetery behind the chemical plant, where the sumac is still black from the fire.

I still live in the flat, though the dry cleaners has closed and the building is empty except for me. The trains still shake the teacups, and the light bulbs still swing, but I don't notice them much.

I went to the docks today. Tran wasn't there. A young guy told me Tran had gone back to Vietnam, to some village in the delta where his sister had a farm.

"He leave a message?" I asked.

"No," the kid said. "Just said he was tired of the snow."

I sat on the pallets by myself, drinking my coffee, watching the cranes. There were only twelve of them this year, a ragged line that circled the harbor twice before heading south. They looked small against the container ships, their white wings gray with the city's soot, but they flew anyway.

"Ma," I whispered.

The trains roared on the bridge overhead, their iron wheels screaming against the rails, and though I knew it was too hot for snow, I ran in the yard, mouth agape, straining to catch the ashes on his tongue. I should've said a proper goodbye, though it's not goodbye, just migration season.
