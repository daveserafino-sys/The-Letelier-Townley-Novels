Transposition

Donna gets pulled over on Route 9, just past the oil tanks, where the road narrows to two lanes and the sumac grows so thick it scratches the passenger side mirror. The cop is a young trooper with a face like a clean potato and a hat that sits so low his ears stick out.

"License and registration, ma'am."

Donna reaches into her purse. She's fifty-two, with graying hair and a pale, serious face, and she's been driving this road since she was sixteen, but she still feels a flutter in her chest when the blue lights wink in her rearview. She hands him her license.

The trooper examines the card, then looks at her. "This license is for a David Serafino," he says.

"Yes," Donna says. "That's me."

"David Serafino is a man, ma'am."

"I am aware of that," she says. "But the license is mine. I had a transposition."

~

The transposition was a corporate service, provided by her employer, the logistics park across the creek. "A seamless transition," the brochure had promised. "Minimize downtime, maximize efficiency." They'd taken her into a room on the third floor of the main warehouse, a clean space with white tile floors and three spotlights on the ceiling. They'd put a helmet on her head and a copper rod in her hands, and then the lights went out.

When the lights came back, she was Donna.

David was still there, she supposed, in some warehouse in Istanbul or Byzantium, but she was here, in David's station wagon, with David's license and David's library card. She was fifty-two, but her joints felt like thirty, and her hair was longer than she'd ever kept it.

She'd gone home to David's apartment. It was a messy place, the living room cluttered with boxes of old books, the kitchen smelling of cabbage and stale grease. She'd spent the weekend cleaning, throwing out ten bags of moldy oranges and old lasagna, washing the windows until she could see the highway over the canyon.

But the license was a problem.

The trooper called for backup. A second car arrived, its tires crunching on the dry gravel of the shoulder. The second cop was older, with a gray mustache and a stomach that hung over his belt like a sack of grain. He looked at her license, then at her car.

"You're driving a David's car, ma'am," he said.

"It's my car," Donna said. "The registration is in the glove box."

"The registration's for David," the older cop said. "We're going to have to impound the vehicle."

"You can't impound it," Donna said. "I have an agenda. I have to clean the gutters today. If I don't clean them, the rain will compromise the flashing."

"What flashing?" the trooper asked.

"The zinc flashing," she said. "On the chimney. It's very old."

~

They took her to the station, a brick building behind the chemical plant that smelled of floor wax and cold coffee. They sat her in an interview room with a wooden table and two steel chairs, under a spotlight that made her head ache.

The older cop, whose name tag said Miller, sat opposite her. He had her purse on the table, its contents spilled out: her keys, her compact, three green apples, and ten index cards from the library.

"What are these?" Miller asked, pointing at the cards.

"They're for my book," Donna said. "A biography of Nuwan."

"Who's Nuwan?"

"He's a librarian," she said. "He works for a company in Istanbul. He was the hundred and eighty-second reader."

Miller looked at the trooper, who was leaning against the door, chewing a toothpick. "We got a live one," Miller said.

They kept her for three hours, running her fingerprints, calling the logistics park, checking her records in five different jurisdictions. The fingerprints belonged to David, but the photograph in the database was a blank square. The logistics park said they had no record of a David Serafino, or a Donna, but they did have a contract for a social developer named And3-04 who had recently been decommissioned.

"Who's your doctor, ma'am?" Miller asked.

"I don't have a doctor," Donna said. "But I have an appointment in eight years. The wait list is very long."

"You need a doctor now," Miller said.

They let her go at midnight. They didn't give her the car back, so she had to walk. Route 9 was dark, the only light coming from the flare at the chemical plant, a blue eye that winked behind the sumac. The air was cold and smelled of sulfur and wet leaves.

She walked with a steady, loose stride, her boots clicking on the asphalt. She felt thin, lighter than she had in years, as if the police had kept some of her weight in their file cabinets.

At the bridge over the canyon, she stopped to look down. The creek was dry, just a line of white stones under the moon, but she could hear the water anyway, a low, musical whir that sounded like a music box in a drawer.

A figure was standing at the end of the bridge. It was tall, shorn, wearing a duffel bag on its shoulder.

"Timothy?" she asked.

The figure turned. Its eyes were amber, blinking in a slow, rhythmic pattern. "No," it said. Its voice was a cello played in an empty room. "I am Craig. I am looking for my well."

"The well's dry, Craig," Donna said.

"That is satisfactory," the robot said. He offered her a green apple from his pocket. "The dry well is more conducive to sleep. Would you care to join me?"

"No," she said. "I have to walk."

She started across the bridge, her slippers silent on the concrete. The wind was from the south, warm and wet, and though she knew it was too hot for snow, she ran in the yard, mouth agape, straining to catch the ashes on his tongue.

"Are you okay?" the robot called after her.

"Yeah," she says. "I feel like a dream."
