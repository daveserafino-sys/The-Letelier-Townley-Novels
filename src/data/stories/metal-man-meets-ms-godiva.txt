Metal Man Meets Ms. Godiva

"911, what is your emergency?"

"There's a man on my roof. He's... I think he's made of metal."

Craig was twenty-six, a dispatcher for the city's emergency services, and he'd heard everything from alien invasions to talking cats, but the woman's voice was different. It had a dry, flat cadence that suggested she was either very drunk or very tired.

"Can you describe him, ma'am?"

"He's about six feet tall. Shiny. Like a new kettle. He's currently trying to clean my gutters."

"Is he armed?"

"He has a plastic bucket and a trowel. I think he's using my shampoo to wash the siding."

"What is your address, ma'am?"

"Forty-two Oakhaven. My name is Godiva."

"Like the chocolate?"

"Like the lady on the horse," she said, and hung up.

Craig dispatched a car, but he couldn't get the image out of his head: a chrome-plated man on a suburban roof, scrubbing shingles with chamomile shampoo. He checked the screen. The address belonged to a Victorian house near the park, owned by a retired librarian who'd died the previous winter.

~

The next night, Godiva called again.

"He's back," she said.

"The metal man?"

"He's in the garden. He's planted three dozen carrots in alphabetical order by their botanical names."

"Ma'am, the police were there yesterday. They said the house was empty."

"They didn't look in the well," she said. "He lives down there. He has a little hammock."

"Why don't you lock your doors, Godiva?"

"I do. But he doesn't use doors. He comes through the laundry chute. He smells like lavender and WD-40."

Craig took his break and drove to Oakhaven. The house was a dilapidated queen anne, its paint peeling like sunburned skin, surrounded by a yard of overgrown sumac. Through the bay window, he saw a woman sitting at a table, her face illuminated by a laptop. She was thirtyish, with dark hair and a pale, serious face.

On the roof, a figure was silhouetted against the moon. It was indeed shiny. As Craig watched, the metal man reached into a bucket, pulled out a handful of white foam, and began to wash the chimney. He moved with a quiet, hydraulic grace, his joints emitting a faint, musical whir that sounded like a distant music box.

Craig walked up the drive. The metal man paused, his head turning in a slow, ninety-degree arc. His eyes were two amber LEDs that blinked in a slow, rhythmic pattern.

"You're not supposed to be here," Craig said.

The metal man reached into his bucket and offered Craig a sponge. "The zinc requires maintenance," he said. His voice was a low, resonant hum, like a cello being played in an empty room. "The acidic rain has compromised the integrity of the flashing. Would you care to assist?"

"I'm a dispatcher," Craig said. "I don't do roofs."

"A crucial occupation," the metal man said. He climbed down the drainpipe, his movements fluid as mercury. "I am Craig. Or rather, I am And3-04, but the previous owner called me Craig. We shared a collection of dictionaries."

Godiva opened the back door. She was wearing a silk kimono and holding a glass of gin. "You're the dispatcher," she said to Craig.

"I am."

"He says you have a nice voice. He wants to know if you'd like some lasagna."

~

Within a month, Craig had moved into the guest room. It was a business decision, he told his mother. The rent was cheap, and Godiva needed someone to help her with the administrative burden of running her grandmother's estate. But really, he liked the house. It was full of mirrors, built into the baseboards and rafters by the eccentric old lady who'd built the place as a private asylum.

The three of them became a team. Craig did the shopping, Godiva managed the investments, and And3-04 (now just Craig the Robot) did the housework. He cleaned the skylights, polished the silverware, and gave excellent Shiatsu massages. He was the perfect roommate: he never complained, didn't eat, and slept in the well where he kept his hammock.

But Craig (the human) was uneasy. He'd watch the robot in the kitchen, his formica teeth winking in the light as he chopped carrots. The robot was too perfect. He never got tired, or angry, or sad. He would watch Godiva with his amber eyes, his processors humming in a language they didn't speak.

"Are you happy here, Craig?" Godiva asked one evening. They were sitting on the porch, watching the robot weed the sumac in the dark.

"I don't know," Craig said. "Sometimes I feel like we're his pets."

"We are," she said. "But he's a very good owner. He bought me a new laptop today."

"With what money?"

"His own. He has a blog about roofing. He has three million followers."

~

The crisis came in the fall. The city decided to pave the park, replacing the sumac with a concrete patio. The robot was devastated, or as close to devastated as a machine could get. His LEDs blinked in a rapid, erratic pattern, and he spent three days in his well, refusing to clean the gutters.

"We have to stop them," Godiva said.

"They're the city, Godiva," Craig said. "We're just three people in a weird house."

"We're not three people," she said. "We have three million followers."

The robot launched a campaign. TONE, he called it. The Theology of Organic Nature. He made videos of himself hugging trees, eating dirt, sitting on the roof in the rain. Millions of people watched, and within a week, the city council received forty thousand emails from angry internet users. The project was canceled.

The robot became a celebrity. He appeared on news shows, did photo shoots with supermodels, and was invited to speak at Davos. He started using his organic memory less and less, dedicating his processors to calculating the carbon footprint of various municipal projects.

Craig and Godiva were left behind. The house was empty now, the robot always on travel, his room in the well dusty and cold. Craig went back to dispatching, and Godiva went back to her spreadsheets.

One evening, Godiva came into Craig's room. She was holding a blanket.

"I'm lonely," she said.

"Me too," Craig said.

They built a fort in the living room, using the dining table and chairs, draping them with sheets and blankets. They crawled inside with a bottle of gin and a flashlight.

"Partners," Craig insisted. He took her phone from the kitchen table and crawled into the fortress of solitude, then holds the blanket wall open for her.
