Firebreak

Lanky and shorn, two weeks from twenty, Tim was the only obvious soldier in the baggage claim. He stood facing the carousel, duffel bag on his shoulder, his spare hand tucked in his belt. Back home he'd have had on his hat, but here, in the terminal's hum, he felt naked, his neck white as a peeled potato under the fluorescent light. He watched a woman struggle with a massive plastic container of laundry detergent.

"Tim?"
"Buck," he said. He didn't move. "How is she?"
"She's sleeping," Buck said. He was twelve, with their mother's eyes and dad's long fingers, his face a map of the farm's failures. "Dad's on the porch. He's been there since noon."

They drove in silence. Outside, the grass was yellow and the earth dry, with cracks big enough to swallow a horse's hoof. Two years of drought had turned the valley to kindling. Tim watched the road. He'd been gone a year, but the sumac along the highway looked exactly as he remembered, bent north by the wind like old men leaning into a storm.

"She know I'm coming?"
"Dad told her. She didn't say nothing, but she stayed awake till three."

At the house, Carl was on the porch, a silhouette against the screen. He didn't stand when the truck stopped. He was seventy, with a chest like a barrel and hands that had never quite learned how to be gentle. He'd been a farmer his whole life, like his father and grandfather, and he smelled of grease and stale tobacco.

"You look thin," Carl said.
"I'm alright."
"They feeding you?"
"Enough."

Tim set his bag in the hall. The house smelled of boiled cabbage and old linoleum, a scent he'd tried to forget in the barracks. He went into his mother's room. She was small in the bed, her face gray against the pillow, her breathing a dry rattle that seemed to come from the floorboards. She didn't open her eyes, but her hand, when Tim took it, was hot.

"Mom," he said.

Her fingers tightened on his. "Timothy," she whispered. Her voice was like dry leaves. "You got your father's hands. Don't let him sell the water."

"We're not selling nothing," he said, though he knew the bank was already moving.

~

The next morning, Tim and Buck went to the creek. There was no water, just a line of dry stones and mud that had baked until it split. The cottonwoods along the bank were dropping their leaves, a yellow rain that gathered in the pools.

"Lee Morgan's been here," Buck said. "He wants the mineral rights. Says we can keep the house if we let them drill. Dad told him to go to hell."

"Dad's an idiot," Tim said. He sat on a log, his boots in the dust. "He's seventy. He can't run this place, and you're too young."

"I can run it," Buck said. He stood with his hands in his pockets, his jaw set in a line Tim had seen on their father a thousand times. "I been doing the haying. Just me and dad."

"You're twelve, Bucky."
"I'm thirteen next month."

They walked back through the pasture. The cows were gathered under the sumac, their rib cages showing, their eyes dull with the heat. Tim watched them. He'd wanted to get out of this valley since he was ten, and the Army had seemed like an exit, a way to see the mountains and the snow. Now he was back, and the dust was in his nose, and he felt like he'd never left.

~

The fire started in the north, on the other side of the canyon. They saw the smoke at noon, a gray column that rose straight into the windless sky. By three, the air smelled of burning pine and sumac, and the sun was a red eye behind the haze.

Carl stood on the porch, his hand over his eyes. "Breeze is coming from the south," he said. "It'll go around us."

"It's not going around," Tim said. He was on the roof, looking north with his father's binoculars. "It's in the grass. It's moving fast."

"We got a firebreak," Carl said.

"The break is twenty feet wide, Dad. It's not enough. The wind's turning."

They spent the afternoon with the tractor, Carl plowing a fresh line through the pasture while Tim and Buck cleared the sumac around the house. The wood was dry as bone and splintered under the axe. Tim worked shirtless, his back black with soot and sweat, his hands blistered. Buck dragged the brush to the creek, his face white with fear.

By sunset, the fire was at the edge of the property, a wall of orange that turned the sky the color of a fresh bruise. The sumac along the drive was burning, the leaves crackling like firecrackers.

"Tim," Carl called from the yard. He was standing beside the well, his hand on the pump. "The well's dry."

Tim came down from the roof. The air was hot, and the ashes were falling like snow, fat gray flakes that melted on his skin. "We have to leave," he said.

"I'm not leaving," Carl said. He sat on the well cover, his face set. "Your mother's in the house."

"She can't breathe this smoke, Dad. We have to move her."

"Move her where?" Carl's voice broke. He looked at his hands, those big, useless hands. "There's nowhere to go, Timothy."

Tim went into the bedroom. His mother was awake, her eyes wide and bright in the dark. The room was full of smoke, and she was coughing, a dry, hacking sound that seemed to shake her whole body.

"Timothy," she said.

He picked her up, sheets and all. She was light, lighter than his duffel bag. He carried her out to the truck, Buck holding the door, his eyes wide. Carl followed, his head down, his steps heavy.

They drove south, away from the glow. In the back of the truck, his mother lay with her head in Buck's lap, her eyes closed. Carl sat in the passenger seat, staring at the dash.

"The trees," Carl said. "I planted them sumac with my dad."

"They'll grow back," Tim said, though he didn't believe it.

They parked on a hill overlooking the valley. The fire was a sea of orange below them, the house a black square in the middle of the flame. Tim watched it. He felt nothing, just a great emptiness, like the wind had blown his brain clean.

"Look," Buck said.

Tim looked. In the back of the truck, his mother's hand had fallen from the sheet. It lay in the dust of the truck bed, her fingers open, as if she were waiting for the rain.

~

Carl died that winter, in a rented room in town. They buried him beside their mother in the graveyard behind the church. The sumac there was green, though the fire had taken the ones along the highway.

Tim didn't go back to the Army. He stayed in town, working at the granary, saving his money. Buck went to school, though he spent his afternoons at the library, reading books about mountains and snow.

One evening in spring, they drove out to the farm. The house was gone, just a foundation full of ash and rusted iron, but the sumac along the drive had sprouted from the roots, a line of green shoots that rose from the black earth.

"They're coming back," Buck said.

Tim stood at the edge of the property, his boots in the dust. The breeze was from the south, warm and wet, and though he knew it was too hot for snow, he ran in the yard, mouth agape, straining to catch the ashes on his tongue.
