We Don't Need No Water

The Jade Palace dumpster is warm and smells of old pork and cabbage. The alley behind the restaurant is narrow, bracketed by brick walls that have turned black from fifty years of kitchen grease, and Route 9 is just a low hum in the distance.

"The grease is thick," Buck says. He's twelve, with a face like a quiet map of the farm, and he's sitting on a crate of rotten cabbage. "It gets in your boots."

"It's not grease, Bucky," Tim says. He's thirteen next month, his lanky arms covered in soot, his eyes wide in the dark. "It's the radon. The dispatcher said there's a leak."

"I like the radon," Buck says. "It keeps the tigers off."

He's lying, of course. He's just scared of the dark, and the alley is full of shadows that look like men in kimonos. He has a dead baby owl in his pocket, a present from his brother, and he keeps his hand on its cold feathers to keep from shaking.

They're looking for food. Their father's well went dry in September, and the bank had moved in by October, leaving them with nothing but the clothes on their backs and the Appaloosa, which they'd had to sell to a man named Lee Morgan for fifty dollars.

They spent the winter in the woods behind the industrial park, living in a shelter made of sumac branches and plastic sheets. It was a hard season, the creek frozen to the mud, the wind from the north smelling of sulfur and burning plastic.

One night, the shelter catches fire.

A spark from their hearth lands on the dry thatch, and within a minute, the structure is a wall of orange that turns the snow to gray muck. They drag their bags to the creek, the dry grass burning behind us, a red wall that rose thirty feet into the dark sky.

"The van's gone," Buck says.

"We don't have a van, Bucky," Tim says. "Just the well."

They walk to Route 9, Tim carrying the water tank on his shoulder like a soldier with a mortar. The air is thick with smoke, and the soot falls on their faces like gray snow, but Buck doesn't cough. He walks with a steady, hydraulic stride, his boots clicking on the asphalt.

At the bridge over the canyon, they stop to look down. A car is parked on the shoulder, its doors open, its headlights winking in the mist. A woman is standing by the rail, her hair white and thin, her face dry as paper.

"Ninh?" she asks.

"No, ma'am," Tim says. "I'm Timothy. This is Buck."

"I thought you were someone else," she says. "The soldier from New Maldives."

"My brother's a soldier," Buck says. "He's in Abu Dhabi."

The woman, whose name tag says Carrie, looks at their black faces and the water tank. "The well's dry, Timothy," she says.

"We know," Tim says.

"The company's records span eighteen years," she says. "They're going to build a warehouse here. A clean space with white tile floors and three spotlights. You'll have to move."

"We're not moving," Tim says. "This is our pasture."

They spent the spring in the cemetery behind the chemical plant. The sumac there was green, though the fire had taken the ones along the drive, and the graves were covered in dandelions that tasted of honey and dirt.

They built a new house, using the bricks from the old foundation and the iron beams from the warehouse. It was a small place, three rooms with linoleum floors and windows that overlook the elevated train, but it was theirs.

One evening, their father came home with a young woman in a pink tutu.

"This is Godiva," Carl said. "She's our new secretary."

"Like the chocolate?" Buck asked.

"Like the lady on the horse," Godiva said. She sat on the well cover, her rings glinting in the sun, and began to chop carrots with a chrome-plated knife. "The well's dry, Carl. We need to buy some zinc."

"We're not buying nothing," Carl said. He sat on his bass case, his hand over his eyes. "The insurance is paid. Let it burn."

They sat in the yard, watching the fire rise from the canyon, a red wall that turned the sky the color of a fresh bruise, and though they knew it was too hot for snow, they ran in the yard, mouth agape, straining to catch the ashes on his tongue. It was like waiting for a train that never came, a troll crouched under the bridge to frighten children and tax the townspeople.
